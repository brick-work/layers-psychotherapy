What Is Therapy Actually For?

Most people come to therapy because something is wrong. A relationship is falling apart. Anxiety has become unmanageable. Something happened that they can't stop thinking about. That's a completely legitimate reason to start — and if that's where you are, you're in the right place.

But the people who tend to get the most out of therapy aren't always the ones in the most pain. They're the ones who are ready to understand something. There's a difference.

"Therapy isn't just about fixing what's broken. It's about understanding how things got this way — and deciding what you want to do about it."

What therapy is not

Therapy is not advice. A good therapist isn't going to tell you what to do with your life, whether to leave your relationship, or how to handle your boss. If that's what you're looking for, I understand — but that's not what the work is.

Therapy is also not a place to just vent, though venting can be part of it. The goal isn't to feel heard and then go home and do the same thing again next week. The goal is movement.

What it actually is

At its best, therapy is a space to slow down and look carefully at something you've been moving too fast to see clearly. It's collaborative — meaning you're not a passenger. You're the one doing the work. I'm here to help you figure out what the work is, and how to do it.

Some people need a few sessions to get to the bottom of something that's been bothering them for years. Others are in it for the long haul — building, layer by layer, a clearer picture of themselves and what they want. Both are valid. Both are welcome here.

The first appointment matters a lot to me. I want you to leave feeling like you want to come back — not because you have to, but because something clicked.

If any of this resonates, reach out. The first step is just a conversation.
